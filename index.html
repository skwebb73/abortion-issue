THE ABORTION ISSUE

The U.S. Supreme Court overturned Roe v. Wade on June 24, 2022.

A wave of protests swept across the nation, bringing to the foreground long-standing debates.

The Daily Tar Heel published a special edition of its weekly newspaper to highlight the impacts of these debates on Orange County and UNC.

All of the content for this issue was created by female and nonbinary members of the newsroom.

  1. North Carolina Criminalizes Abortion

    1881

    North Carolina criminalized abortion and the concealing of birth of a child. It became a felony for people to administer procedures to induce an abortion. This law was cited in the 2022 Supreme Court’s ruling to overturn Roe v. Wade.

  2. Health risk exception

    1967

    The N.C. General Assembly added a provision that doctors in a hospital may perform abortion procedures if there is a substantial health risk to the life of the pregnant person or if the child would be burn with a serious physical or mental defect. Doctors may also perform abortions for pregnancies that resulted in rape if the rape was reported to law enforcement agencies within seven days. Three doctors would have to then examine the pregnant person and certify in writing that they believe the abortion is justified.

  3. Roe v. Wade

    1973

    o In January 1973, the U.S. Supreme Court’s decision on Roe v. Wade guarantees an individual’s right to abortion. A few months later in May 1973, North Carolina outlines that abortion is no longer unlawful if performed during the first 20 weeks of a pregnancy. If there is substantial risk to the pregnant person, an abortion may be performed by a licensed physician.

  4. State abortion fund

    1978

    The state abortion fund was established to aid low-income individuals in paying for abortion services. Later laws in 1995 made the fund effectively unusable with an increase in restrictions, and in 2006 the fund got repealed.

  5. Parental consent required

    1995

    Parental consent is not required for abortion care, except in medical abortions. It became a Class 1 misdemeanor for a doctor to perform an abortion on a minor without parental consent.

  6. Restrictions to access

    2011

    The “Woman’s Right to Know Act” restricts abortion access in the state by enacting a mandatory 24-hour waiting period, state-mandated counseling, and forced ultrasounds in which the patient must view the ultrasound images. The ultrasound requirement was later struck down in Stuart v. Huff the same year.

  7. More legislation restricts access

    2013

    The NC General Assembly continued to make it harder for people to access abortions. It passed new requirements that made it harder for women’s health centers to stay open and prohibited Affordable Care Act health insurance from going towards abortion coverage except in rare cases of rape, incest, or risk of life.

  8. Women and Children Protection Act

    2015

    This act increased the waiting period for abortions from 24 hours to 72 hours. It also required the NC Department of Health and Human Services to collect medical and demographic records on abortions.

  9. 20-week ban ruled unlawful

    2019

    The federal district court declared the 20-week ban unlawful and extended abortion access up to viability. Upon the overturning of Roe v. Wade, this injunction was overturned.

  10. Roe v. Wade overturned, N.C. restricts access

    2022

    On June 24, 2022, the U.S. Supreme Court overturned Roe v. Wade, effectively ending federal protections to the right of abortion. Later in August, a U.S. District Court reinstated the 20-week ban on abortion in North Carolina, except for exceptions for medical emergencies.

What does this mean for North Carolina?

  • Abortion is a key issue of the 2022 midterm election in which Republicans need just three more seats in the House and two more seats in the Senate to take the supermajority.
  • There are 14 clinics in North Carolina that provide abortions.
  • North Carolina has some of the most liberal abortion laws of the southern states right now. Abortion is fully illegal in Tennessee and is banned after six weeks of pregnancy in Georgia.
  • In Orange County, 253 abortions were performed in 2020.
